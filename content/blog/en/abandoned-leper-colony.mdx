Perched dramatically on the volcanic cliffs above Abades, the **Sanatorio de Abona** is one of the most striking and mysterious landmarks in Tenerife. Its crumbling white walls, empty corridors and panoramic sea views make it an unforgettable sight — and its history is as fascinating as its appearance.

## What is the Sanatorio de Abona?

The Sanatorio de Abona was built in the **1930s and 1940s** as a tuberculosis hospital. At the time, tuberculosis (TB) was one of the deadliest diseases in Europe, and doctors believed that **clean sea air, sunshine and a warm climate** were among the best treatments available.

The location above Abades was chosen deliberately — the elevated position above the sea ensured constant fresh Atlantic breezes, while the south-facing aspect guaranteed maximum sunshine throughout the year. The Canary Islands, and Tenerife in particular, had become a destination for European TB patients seeking recovery in the early 20th century.

### The Architecture

The building is a striking example of **rationalist architecture** from the 1930s — clean lines, large windows designed to maximise light and ventilation, and long open terraces where patients would rest in the sun and sea air. Even in its ruined state, the scale and ambition of the original structure is impressive.

The complex included:
- **Patient wards** with large windows facing the sea
- **Treatment rooms** and medical facilities
- **Staff quarters** and administrative buildings
- **Outdoor terraces** with unobstructed Atlantic views
- **A chapel** for patients and staff
- **Isolation wards** for the most severe cases

On clear days, the views from the sanatorio stretch all the way to the neighbouring island of Gran Canaria.

## The History

### Construction (1934–1943)

Construction began in **1934** under the direction of Spanish architect **José Enrique Marrero Regalado**. The project was ambitious and expensive, funded by the Spanish government as part of a nationwide campaign to combat tuberculosis.

The building was designed to house **up to 300 patients** and a full medical staff. The architecture reflected the latest thinking in TB treatment — maximum exposure to fresh air and sunlight, with patients spending most of their time on open-air terraces.

### The Tuberculosis Era

The sanatorio opened in the **early 1940s** and operated as a TB hospital for approximately **two decades**. Patients came from across the Canary Islands and mainland Spain, seeking the healing climate of Tenerife's south coast.

Treatment was rudimentary by modern standards:
- **Bed rest** in the open air
- **Sunbathing** (heliotherapy)
- **Nutritious diet** to build strength
- **Surgical procedures** including lung collapse therapy
- **Isolation** from the general population

Life in the sanatorio was regimented and often lasted months or even years. Patients lived in a self-contained world, cut off from their families and normal life.

### Decline and Abandonment

By the **1950s**, the discovery of effective antibiotic treatments for tuberculosis — particularly **streptomycin** — revolutionised TB care. Patients could now be cured with medication rather than months of isolation in sanatoria.

The need for dedicated TB hospitals disappeared almost overnight. The Sanatorio de Abona was gradually **abandoned in the 1960s** as patients were discharged and no new admissions arrived.

The building was left to the elements. Over the following decades, the structure fell into disrepair. The roof collapsed, windows shattered, and the Atlantic wind and salt air accelerated the decay.

### Modern Day

Today, the sanatorio stands as a **haunting ruin** — a monument to a disease that once terrified Europe and to the patients who spent their final days here. The site has become popular with:
- **Urban explorers** and photographers
- **History enthusiasts** interested in medical history
- **Film and TV productions** (it's been featured in several Spanish films)
- **Tourists** seeking something off the beaten path

## What You'll See Today

### The Main Building

The central structure is the most intact part of the complex. You can still see:
- **Long corridors** with peeling paint and broken tiles
- **Patient rooms** with sea-facing windows
- **The main staircase** leading to upper floors (unsafe to climb)
- **Treatment rooms** with remnants of medical equipment
- **The chapel** with its distinctive arched entrance

### The Terraces

The open-air terraces where patients would rest are still visible, though overgrown and crumbling. The views from here are extraordinary — the entire south-east coast of Tenerife stretches out below.

### The Outbuildings

Scattered around the main building are:
- **Staff quarters** — smaller buildings in various states of collapse
- **Storage buildings** and workshops
- **Water tanks** that supplied the complex
- **The old access road** winding up from Abades village

### Graffiti and Art

Over the years, the sanatorio has been covered in graffiti — some of it crude, but some surprisingly artistic. The juxtaposition of street art against the decaying architecture creates a strange, post-apocalyptic atmosphere.

## Visiting the Sanatorio de Abona

### Location

The sanatorium is located on the cliffs above Abades village, about a **10-minute walk** from Ocean Deck House.

**Address:** Carretera General del Sur (TF-1), Abades, Arico  
**GPS Coordinates:** 28.1633° N, 16.4547° W

### Access

- **Free entry** — the site is open to the public
- **No guided tours** — it's a self-guided exploration
- **Open 24/7** — though daytime visits are strongly recommended
- **No facilities** — no toilets, water, or shade

### How to Get There

**From Ocean Deck House:**
1. Walk up the hill from Abades beach (10 min)
2. Follow the old road towards the TF-1 highway
3. The sanatorio is visible on the clifftop to your right

**By car:**
- Park in Abades village and walk up (safest option)
- Or park on the roadside near the sanatorio (limited space, watch for traffic)

### What to Bring

- **Sturdy shoes** — lots of uneven ground, broken glass, and debris
- **Camera** — incredible photo opportunities
- **Flashlight** — some areas are dark (but do NOT enter closed buildings)
- **Water** — no facilities on-site
- **Sun protection** — little shade, can be very hot
- **Long trousers** — protection from sharp edges and plants

## ⚠️ Safety Considerations

**Important:** The sanatorio is a dangerous site. Please take these warnings seriously:

- **The buildings are structurally unsound** — do NOT enter closed buildings or climb stairs
- **Floors and ceilings can collapse** without warning
- **Watch for loose rocks and debris**
- **The cliff edges are dangerous** — stay well back
- **No facilities** (toilets, water, first aid)
- **No mobile phone signal** in some areas
- **Supervise children closely** at all times
- **Avoid visiting alone** — go with at least one other person

**We strongly recommend viewing the exterior only.** The most dramatic views and photo opportunities are from outside the buildings.

## 📸 Photography Tips

The Sanatorio de Abona is a photographer's dream:

### Best Shots

1. **The main facade** against the blue sky
2. **Arched doorways** framing ocean views
3. **Abandoned corridors** with peeling paint
4. **Graffiti art** on crumbling walls

- **Golden hour (sunrise/sunset)** — warm, dramatic light
- **Midday** — harsh light but good for black and white
- **Overcast days** — moody, atmospheric shots

### Drone Photography

Drone photography is permitted but be aware of:
- Strong winds on the clifftop
- Proximity to the TF-1 highway
- Respect for other visitors

## The Cultural Impact

### In Popular Culture

The sanatorio has appeared in:
- **Spanish films** and TV series
- **Photography exhibitions** worldwide
- **Urban exploration documentaries**
- **Music videos** by Spanish and international artists

### Local Attitudes

Locals have mixed feelings about the site:
- Some see it as a **tragic waste** and eyesore
- Others view it as an important **historical monument**
- There are occasional **ghost stories** (mostly exaggerated for tourists)
- Some want it **restored**, others prefer it left as a ruin

### Conservation Debate

There's ongoing debate about the sanatorio's future:
- **Demolition** — some argue it's dangerous and should be torn down
- **Restoration** — others want it preserved as a museum or cultural centre
- **Stabilisation** — make it safe while maintaining its ruined character
- **Status quo** — leave it as it is

For now, it remains in limbo — neither protected nor demolished.

## Nearby Attractions

After visiting the sanatorio, explore:

- **Playa de Abades** — beautiful beach (5 min walk downhill)
- **Abades village** — authentic fishing village with local restaurants
- **Coastal hiking trails** along the cliffs
- **Arico Nuevo** — traditional Canarian village (10 min drive)

## Ethical Tourism

When visiting abandoned sites like this, remember:

- **Leave no trace** — take only photos, leave only footprints
- **Respect the history** — this was a place of suffering and healing
- **Don't vandalize** — graffiti damages the site for future visitors
- **Support local businesses** — visit Abades village and eat at local restaurants
- **Share responsibly** — don't encourage dangerous behavior in your photos

## Conclusion

The Sanatorio de Abona is more than just an abandoned building — it's a **time capsule of medical history**, a testament to the devastating impact of tuberculosis, and a reminder of how quickly medical science can transform our world.

Whether you're a history buff, urban explorer, photographer, or simply curious traveler, the sanatorio offers a unique and thought-provoking experience.
