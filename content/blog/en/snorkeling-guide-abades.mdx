Abades is one of Tenerife's best-kept secrets for snorkeling. The sheltered bay, crystal-clear Atlantic water, and volcanic rock formations create a natural underwater world that rivals anything you'll find at the busier resort beaches. And the best part? **It's right on your doorstep from Ocean Deck House.**

## Why Abades is Perfect for Snorkeling

The bay at Abades is naturally protected from the trade winds that batter the rest of the south coast. This means the water is almost always **calm, flat and clear** — even when it's choppy elsewhere. Visibility regularly exceeds 15 metres.

The volcanic rock shelves that line the bay create natural reefs, crevices and overhangs that are home to an extraordinary variety of marine life. Unlike sandy resort beaches, the rocky bottom here means **fish, eels and octopus are everywhere**.

### What Makes It Special

- **Crystal-clear visibility** — often exceeding 15–20 meters
- **Calm, protected waters** — ideal for beginners and families
- **Marine protected area** — thriving ecosystem with abundant life
- **Easy shore access** — no boat needed, walk straight in
- **Uncrowded** — away from the tourist beaches
- **Free** — no entrance fees or permits required

## 🐠 What You'll See

The marine life in Abades is surprisingly diverse for such a small bay. Here's what you can expect to encounter:

### Common Sightings

- **Parrotfish** — the colourful stars of Abades bay, often seen grazing on algae
- **Ornate wrasse** — curious and friendly, often swimming right up to you
- **Moray eels** — hiding in rock crevices, harmless if not provoked
- **Octopus** — masters of camouflage, look carefully in the rocks
- **Sea urchins** — abundant on the rocky bottom (watch where you step!)
- **Damselfish, bream, and salema** — schooling in large groups
- **Starfish and sea cucumbers** — on the sandy patches between rocks

### Lucky Sightings

- **Green sea turtles** — occasional visitors, especially in summer
- **Rays** — eagle rays and stingrays glide over the sandy bottom
- **Barracuda** — solitary hunters patrolling the deeper water
- **Trumpet fish** — long, slender fish that hover vertically
- **Cuttlefish** — incredible colour-changing abilities

## 📍 Best Snorkeling Spots in Abades

### 1. The Main Bay (Playa de Abades)

**Difficulty:** Beginner  
**Entry:** Easy sandy beach entry  
**Depth:** 1–4 metres  
**Best for:** Families, first-time snorkelers

The main beach is the easiest entry point. Enter from the sandy area and swim left (south) along the rocky shelf. The rocks start almost immediately and the marine life begins within metres of the shore.

**What to see:** Parrotfish, wrasse, damselfish, sea urchins, and occasionally octopuses hiding in crevices.

### 2. The Northern Rocky Point

**Difficulty:** Intermediate  
**Entry:** Rocky shore (wear water shoes)  
**Depth:** 3–6 metres  
**Best for:** Experienced snorkelers, underwater photography

Swim north from the beach towards the rocky headland. The water gets slightly deeper here and the rock formations are more dramatic with caves and overhangs.

**What to see:** Moray eels, larger groupers, schools of bream, and the occasional ray.

### 3. The Southern Cove

**Difficulty:** Beginner–Intermediate  
**Entry:** Short walk over rocks  
**Depth:** 1–3 metres  
**Best for:** Spotting octopus and flatfish

A small, secluded cove south of the main beach accessible by a short walk over the rocks. Often completely deserted. The shallow water over a mixed sand and rock bottom is perfect for spotting camouflaged creatures.

**What to see:** Octopus, flatfish, juvenile fish, starfish, sea cucumbers.

## 🏊 What Gear to Bring

### Essential

- **Mask and snorkel** — bring your own for hygiene and proper fit
- **Fins** — highly recommended for easier swimming and safety
- **Water shoes or reef shoes** — the volcanic rock can be sharp
- **Rash vest or wetsuit** — sun protection and warmth (water is 18–24°C year-round)

### Recommended

- **Underwater camera** — GoPro or waterproof phone case
- **Dry bag** — for valuables on the beach
- **Snorkel vest** — extra buoyancy and visibility (especially for beginners)
- **Anti-fog spray** — for your mask
- **Reef-safe sunscreen** — protect your skin and the marine environment

## 🌊 Best Time to Snorkel

### Time of Day

**Morning (8:00 AM – 11:00 AM):** The water is calmest, visibility is best, and marine life is most active. The sun angle is also perfect for underwater photography.

**Avoid:** Afternoons when winds pick up, and midday when the sun is directly overhead (harder to see underwater).

### Best Months

**May to October:** Warmest water temperatures (22–25°C), calmest conditions, best visibility.

**November to April:** Cooler water (18–21°C) but still excellent snorkeling. Wetsuit recommended.

### Weather Conditions

Check the weather before you go:
- **Wind:** South or west winds are best; avoid strong north or east winds
- **Swell:** Look for calm days with swell under 1 metre
- **Visibility:** Best on sunny days with no recent rain

## 🦺 Safety Tips

### Before You Enter

- Never snorkel alone — always have a buddy
- Check weather and sea conditions
- Tell someone on shore your plan
- Know your limits — don't swim too far out
- Watch for boat traffic near the small harbour

### In the Water

- Stay close to shore if you're a beginner
- Don't touch or disturb marine life
- Watch out for sea urchins on rocks
- If you feel tired, float on your back and rest
- Keep an eye on currents — they're usually minimal but can pick up

### Emergency

- Nearest lifeguard: Playa de la Tejita (15 min drive)
- Emergency number: **112**
- Nearest hospital: Hospital del Sur, Arona (25 min drive)

## 🌍 Environmental Responsibility

Abades bay is part of a marine protected area. Help keep it pristine:

- **Don't touch** coral, rocks, or marine life
- **Don't feed** the fish — it disrupts their natural behaviour
- **Take all trash** with you (or better yet, pick up any you see)
- **Use reef-safe sunscreen** — chemical sunscreens harm marine ecosystems
- **Don't collect** shells, rocks, or marine life
- **Respect nesting areas** — some beaches nearby have turtle nesting sites

- **Bar La Esquina** — local tapas and cold beer

Try the **pescado fresco a la plancha** (grilled fresh fish) or **pulpo a la gallega** (Galician-style octopus).

## 📸 Photography Tips

Abades offers excellent opportunities for underwater photography:

- **Best light:** Morning (8–10 AM) for clear, bright conditions
- **Get close:** Water reduces colour and clarity — get as close as possible
- **Shoot upwards:** Capture fish silhouettes against the bright surface
- **Look for details:** Macro shots of octopus eyes, fish scales, and coral textures
- **Be patient:** Wait for fish to come to you rather than chasing them
